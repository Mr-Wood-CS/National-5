# Design Notation

Design notation is a way of showing the design of a program before it is coded. It helps programmers plan the steps, decisions and loops needed to solve a problem.

!!! info "What you Need to Know"

    Describe, identify, and be able to read and understand:
    
    * structure diagrams
            
    * flowcharts
            
    * pseudocode
    
    Exemplify and implement one of the above design notations to design efficient solutions to a problem.

## What is Design Notation?

Design notation is the method used to represent the design of a program. It lets a programmer show how a solution will work without writing the final code.

At National 5 level, design notation is used to show the three programming constructs:

* **Sequencing** - instructions carried out in order
* **Selection** - a decision or choice
* **Iteration** - repetition or looping

There are three main types of design notation used in this section:

* **Structure diagrams** provide a visual breakdown of a solution, showing how a large problem can be divided into smaller, more manageable tasks.

* **Flowcharts** illustrate the logical flow of processes, making it easier to follow the sequence of steps, decisions and actions within an algorithm.
  
* **Pseudocode** bridges the gap between natural language and programming by offering a simplified, language-independent way to represent a solution before it is coded.

!!! tip "Exam Tip"

    In the National 5 course, you may be asked to identify, describe, read or create design notation. Make sure you understand what each notation looks like and when it should be used.

## Why is Design Notation Important?

Good design notation helps programmers:

* plan solutions before writing code
* identify errors and missing requirements early
* communicate ideas with other developers and stakeholders
* create programs that are easier to test, maintain and improve

Throughout this section, you will learn how sequencing, selection and iteration can be represented using structure diagrams, flowcharts and pseudocode.
